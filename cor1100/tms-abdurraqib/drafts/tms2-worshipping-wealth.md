## What Hanley's Ch. IX actually argues (read directly from the chapter PDF)

Epigraph (Smith, public domain): "This disposition to admire, and almost to worship,
the rich and the powerful, and to despise, or, at least, to neglect persons of poor and
mean condition, though necessary both to establish and to maintain the distinction of
ranks and the order of society, is, at the same time, the great and most universal cause
of the corruption of our moral sentiments."

Hanley's gloss (paraphrased, not quoted at length — his 2019 commentary is copyrighted):
Smith is unusually willing to hold both sides of this at once — the same impulse that
keeps society ordered is also what corrupts it. Hanley reads two distinct costs:

1. **Individual cost** — chasing the attention and distinction the world rewards
   sacrifices the inner tranquility we actually need. There's a structural mismatch
   between what the world incentivizes and what we need to be okay.
2. **Social cost** — admiring the rich doesn't just make us overlook the poor (already
   covered in earlier chapters); Smith's language ("neglect," "despise") suggests it
   tips into something closer to contempt. Hanley flags this as in direct tension with
   the opening line of TMS itself — the claim that we're naturally interested in others'
   happiness.

Hanley brings in Rousseau's *Emile* as a contemporary parallel: modern life splits us
between what nature wants and what society rewards, leaving us unable to be "good either
for ourselves or for others." The chapter ends on a deliberate cliffhanger — having
diagnosed the problem, it sets up Smith's proposed remedy in the next chapter (X, "On
Friendship").

## The Abdurraqib passage (p. 65, There's Always This Year)

"You get what I mean now about the things people believe you deserve to have. It isn't
just about the car or the shoes or the platinum chain as objects, though it is a little
about that, too. It's about a history of America selling dreams back to its people for
so long that they stopped knowing what to do when someone they wanted to keep at arm's
length also got to buy into the fantasy."

Context (from the RARI guide's framing, not verified against the surrounding pages
myself yet): this sits inside Abdurraqib's reflection on what success is allowed to look
like, and who is allowed to want it without suspicion.

## Why this pairing works

Both texts are making the same move: wealth/success isn't just an individual good, it's
a *story sold to a population*, and the act of wanting it (or being seen wanting it)
changes how people relate to each other — alienation, contempt, suspicion, "arm's
length." Smith's version is abstract and structural (ranks, order of society). Abdurraqib's
version is concrete and particular (a platinum chain, a specific resentment about who's
"allowed" to buy into the fantasy). That's exactly the kind of move the TMS rubric already
asks for — "connects his argument to something from Hanley" — except now there's a third
text doing the concretizing work that Hanley's commentary doesn't.

## Draft prompt direction (not final)

Something like: *Smith says our worship of wealth corrupts us by making us either
neglect or despise the poor. Abdurraqib describes a different but related corruption —
the suspicion aimed at people who want the dream but aren't supposed to be allowed it.
Where do these two accounts agree, and where does Abdurraqib's version complicate or
push back on Smith's?*

A weak response would just restate both passages. A strong one has to locate the actual
disagreement or gap — e.g., Smith frames the corrupted relationship as flowing from the
rich/order downward; Abdurraqib's passage centers on suspicion flowing sideways, between
people who all want the same thing. That gap is the productive friction for a TMS
response.
