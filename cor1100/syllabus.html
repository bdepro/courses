Section 2 Why Should You Take This Course?

Adam Smith thought about questions most introductory courses skip. How do we actually become the kind of people worth being? Our Great Purpose is Hanley’s guide to what Smith argued in the Theory of Moral Sentiments, and it is worth reading on its own terms.

You will spend the semester reading it carefully, arguing with it in writing, and explaining what you think out loud. Push back where you disagree. When Smith’s argument strains against something from your own life, that tension is worth examining.

Smith is one way of seeing. History, economics, psychology, and sociology illuminate things philosophy cannot. But learning to read a philosophical argument seriously, and to question it, is worth the semester.

Section 3 What Will You Be Able to Do?
  • 1 Read a philosophical argument closely (what it claims, what it offers as evidence, what it leaves out) and say what you think it gets right.
  • 2 Write and speak in plain language about ideas that are not simple, without using jargon as a substitute for thinking.
  • 3 Apply Smith’s and Hanley’s arguments to situations from your own life and explain whether the argument holds.
  • 4 Track analytical threads across texts and over time (sympathy, self-command, the impartial spectator) and see how each one develops and complicates the others.
  • 5 Argue honestly in writing and in the viva by pushing back on a text, asking what it leaves out, and explaining your disagreements directly.

Mission Statement

The Core Curriculum embodies the university’s mission: it prepares you to be a global citizen and informed leader motivated by concern for the common good. The learning goals of the Elon Core Curriculum represent characteristics of a liberal arts education and will serve you throughout your lifetime. It does this by giving all Elon students—regardless of major and minor fields of study—shared courses and experiences that put knowledge into practice and enable the integration of learning across the disciplines.

Core 1100 Themes
  1. The analysis and evaluation of personal and social responsibility
  2. The processes, limitations, and implications of ethical reasoning
  3. Diversity and its relationship to intercultural competency
  4. The relationship between humans and the natural world
  5. The influence of power and resistance in historical and contemporary interactions
  6. The impact of globalization in an increasingly connected, technological, and rapidly changing world
What will you be able to do by the end of this course?

Curiosity & Questioning: The identification of significant issues and the framing of relevant questions.

  • The student can identify a relevant or significant topic or issue to investigate.
  • The student can pose probing questions related to a topic or issue.

Global Perspective: An understanding of the interconnectedness of the human experience within and across cultures and environments.

  • The student can identify impacts of globalization in an increasingly connected, technological, and rapidly changing world.
  • The student can recognize that different perspectives and worldviews reflect diverse backgrounds and experiences.

Critical Thinking: The analysis, interpretation, evaluation, and synthesis of information from multiple sources: oral, visual, and written

  • The student can evaluate the local or global consequences of his/her individual choices.
  • The student can recognize the interconnections between human activities and the rest of the natural world.

Communication Skills: The preparation and presentation of ideas and information orally, visually, and in writing.

  • The student can write as a citizen to communicate effectively with other members of academic and non-academic communities.
  • The student can utilize writing to understand new content and perspectives, analyze information and problems, generate knowledge, or make a difference.
  • The student can express ideas and conclusions in various media/modes as appropriate to the intended audience with clarity and reason.
Notes from the COR Curriculum Committee: COR 1100 FAQ
  • Why is each section different? It is a great strength of COR 1100 that different sections have both commonalities and differences. As you discuss the world with your classmates and faculty across campus, you’ll have common threads for discussion yet different experiences to share. All sections of COR 1100 address common learning goals and global themes, and COR 1100 professors meet on a weekly basis to coordinate and exchange ideas. Nevertheless, there are many ways of addressing the same goals and themes. Thus, different sections of the course often use different texts, assignments, and seminar topics. This balance between commonalities and differences contributes to an intellectually vibrant atmosphere throughout your first year of college.
  • What is a seminar? A seminar is a teaching pedagogy in which the faculty and students engage in collaborative learning through discussion. You are expected to bring questions and ideas to each class and be prepared to discuss them with your classmates. Class discussion is an opportunity to explore new concepts, gain information, and bounce ideas off of your peers. Discussion also has the significant role of challenging, enhancing, and even supporting, at times, your own beliefs.
  • Why do we write so much? In order to advance intellectually, you must be willing to explore what you think and to share it with others. While we will certainly share our ideas in discussions, the core of the class is your thinking, and the goal of the class is developing this thinking to its fullest. Thus, I will ask you to write often, some informal, some formal, so that you can both reflect upon what you think and see your own thinking develop over the course of the class. Some of your writing will be messy, incomplete, and unsatisfying. Other pieces will give you more time to develop your thinking, resulting in a satisfying, complete project.

Section 6 How Will Your Learning Be Assessed?
Check-Ins

You will take four in-class check-ins, each on a Wednesday. Check-In 4 always counts. If your CI 4 score beats your lowest score from Check-Ins 1, 2, or 3, I replace that score automatically. You do not need to ask. Miss a check-in and CI 4 becomes your makeup. There are no separate makeup exams.

You may bring one 5×8 index card with notes to each check-in.

If 75% of the class completes the end-of-semester survey, everyone gets one bonus point on Check-In 4.

Friday Focus

Friday Focus asks one question: what do Hanley and Smith say about something you actually care about? You spend the semester building your answer.

Part 1 — The Narrative

Write the narrative and submit it on Canvas by Wednesday, December 2 at 11:59 p.m. It is a written argument, for a reader who has never taken this course. Three Fridays are set aside for that purpose. Come to those classes with something on the page.

Part 2 — The Narrative Viva

The viva is a short conversation with me about what you wrote. Walk me through your argument and explain the choices you made. Sign up for a slot during viva week; slots fill before Thanksgiving.

Puzzles

One Puzzle per content week, due the following Wednesday at 11:59 p.m. Eight days gives you time to try the puzzle after the reading, sit in Monday’s class, and come back with a sharper answer. Each puzzle asks you to apply the concepts from that week’s Hanley chapters.

You have two attempts. Take the first one after the reading. After Monday’s class, go back to what you missed. Canvas shows your score between attempts. You can use it to figure out where to spend your time.

I drop your lowest puzzle score at the end of the semester. One bad week does not sink your grade. If you miss the Wednesday deadline, you have one more week to submit for 80% credit. After that, the puzzle closes.

There is no Puzzle in Week 7 (Mon–Tue only before Fall Break). That week has a PDF reading but no quiz.

Theory of Moral Sentiments

Four times during the semester you will read a passage from Adam Smith’s Theory of Moral Sentiments and respond to it in writing. I introduce each one on the Friday of the designated week, due the following Wednesday at 11:59 p.m.

A strong response pushes back on the passage, asks what Smith left out, or connects his argument to something from Hanley. A weak response restates what the passage already said.

Section 7 How Can I Use AI in This Class?

AI use in this course is conditional. You can use AI tools to work through material you found confusing or to quiz yourself before assessments. Submitting AI-generated work as your own, or using AI during a restricted assessment, is an Honor Code violation. If you are not sure, ask first.

Section 9 Campus Resources & University Policies
  • Honor Code. Elon’s honor pledge calls for a commitment to Elon’s shared values of Honesty, Integrity, Responsibility, and Respect. Students should review code of conduct policies described in the student handbook. Violations in academic-related areas will be documented in an incident report maintained in the Office of Student Conduct and may result in a lowering of the course grade and/or failure of the course with an Honor Code F. Violations specifically covered by academic honor code policies include plagiarism, cheating, lying, stealing, and the facilitation of another’s dishonesty.
  • Office of Academic Accommodations and Accessibility. Elon University is committed to providing an inclusive experience, accessible learning environments and equal opportunity to students with disabilities in accordance with the Americans with Disabilities Act and Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act. Students needing reasonable accommodations should contact the Office of Academic Accommodations and Accessibility at 336.278.6568 or accommodations@elon.edu. Reasonable accommodations can be requested at any time throughout the semester; however, they are not retroactive. For more information visit the AAA website.
  • Learning Assistance. The Koenigsberger Learning Center offers tutoring, success coaching, and other academic support services. Students are encouraged to visit the Learning Assistance website to view supported courses and explore additional resources. For questions, contact learningassistance@elon.edu.
  • Belk Library. The librarians in Belk Library help students find appropriate resources for course assignments. Students can schedule an appointment or chat with a librarian on the library website.
  • The Writing Center. Located on the first floor of Belk Library, Elon’s Writing Center is staffed by well-trained undergraduate consultants who work with students on any kind of writing at any stage of the writing process. The Writing Center is a welcoming place for all writers, regardless of experience or expertise.
  • Religious Holidays. Elon has a policy and procedures for students who wish to observe religious holidays that conflict with the academic calendar. Students must complete the online Religious Observance Notification Form (RONF) by the date published each semester. Students are required to make prior arrangements with the instructor for completion of any work missed during the absence.
  • Enrollment. Students should confirm their enrollment through their OnTrack account. Students who do not appear on the course roll should consult with their instructor immediately.
  • Dropping a Course. Students may drop a course during the designated drop/add period through OnTrack. A course dropped during the drop/add period will not appear on the student’s transcript.
  • Withdrawing from a Course. After the drop/add period, students may withdraw from a course without penalty before the withdrawal deadline published in the Academic Calendar. Withdrawing will result in a mark of “W” on the student’s transcript. Students may withdraw via OnTrack. Students should not assume they will be officially withdrawn based on failure to attend or notifying a faculty member of intent to withdraw.
  • E-Warnings. E-Warnings are used to communicate with students and advisors when concerns arise about academic performance. Advisors are copied on every e-warning submitted. E-Warnings are part of a standard process and not a personal judgment.
  • Student Care & Outreach. Students who become concerned about a fellow classmate’s well-being are encouraged to share concerns through Elon’s Share a Concern website. See the Student Care and Outreach website for additional resources.
  • Substance Use. Substance use can affect individuals in different ways. Elon offers confidential, nonjudgmental, recovery-affirming support for students who want to make changes to their use of alcohol, cannabis, opioids, or other substances. Peer-led support is available through the Phoenix Free collegiate recovery program, including harm reduction education. Students facing an immediate medical emergency should call 911. Visit the Collegiate Recovery webpage for more information or email phoenixfree@elon.edu.
