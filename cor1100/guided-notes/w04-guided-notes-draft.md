## Common Pitfalls

| Pitfall | What students assume | What the text actually claims | How to avoid it |
|---|---|---|---|
| Collapsing two discrete errors into one | Over-rating the future and under-valuing the present are the same point restated | Hanley presents them as two separate, discrete errors | |
| Concluding Smith condemns ambition outright | The parable means "don't try to get ahead" | Hanley explicitly blocks this — economic growth depends on the same drive | |
| Reading the division-of-labor paradox as a reversal | WN Book 5 contradicts or retracts Book 1's productivity claims | Smith holds both — productivity gains *and* mental costs — simultaneously | |
| Assuming Smith's policy fits his reputation | Smith would favor minimal government, even here | Smith explicitly calls for serious government attention and public-funded education | |
| Treating "sit still" as the full answer | The obvious lesson (slow down) is Hanley's whole point | Hanley flags two further, less-obvious claims as more important | |
| Resolving the middle path by picking a side | "Tranquility and enjoyment together" still requires choosing one | Both are jointly necessary, not a choice | |
