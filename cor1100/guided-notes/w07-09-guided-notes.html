Part V
Common Pitfalls Across This Unit
These are the most common ways students flatten or misapply the four chapters above.
Pitfall What students assume What the text actually claims How to avoid it
Treating earlier self-interest claims as discarded Ch. 1/4/8's claims were simply wrong Hanley frames them as real but incomplete steps toward the deeper claim about love
Conflating attention and love Any expression of affection counts as the same thing Smith draws a sharp having-vs.-being distinction between them
Taking the exchange framing too literally Smith means love can literally be bought and sold Hanley draws a structural analogy ("give to get"), not a claim that love is a commodity
Missing the normative move This chapter is just more description of how people act Hanley's explicit point is that Smith here tells readers how they *should* act
Dismissing the society-of-love vision as merely utopian Idealistic visions aren't worth taking seriously Hanley gives three explicit reasons to take it seriously anyway
Reading the "man of system" warning as abandoning ideals The warning undercuts the whole chapter's argument Smith explicitly affirms ideals are still necessary — the danger is in how they're held
Reading "lovely" in its modern sense "Lovely" means attractive or pleasant Smith means moral worthiness/deservingness specifically
Assuming others' opinions are entirely irrelevant to loveliness "Only conscience can judge loveliness" means others' opinions don't matter at all The narrower claim: others judge attention and love; conscience judges loveliness specifically
