Part IV
Common Pitfalls Across This Week
These are the most common ways students flatten or misapply the three chapters above.
Pitfall What students assume What the text actually claims How to avoid it
Good intentions = sufficient action Feeling bad or wishing well satisfies Smith's standard Smith explicitly denies this — exertion is required
Obligation applies to all of humanity equally Smith's mandate to act for others is unlimited in scope Smith specifies neighbors and those nearby, not distant strangers
"Attention" = vanity or shallowness Wanting to be noticed is simply a character flaw Hanley says Smith merely notes the phenomenon here, without yet moralizing
Imagination-based desires are easier to satisfy Something "imaginary" must be less demanding than something bodily The chapter argues the reverse — imagination's limitlessness makes it harder to cap
Wealth-and-recognition is either all good or all bad Commercial society either liberates everyone or overlooks the poor — pick one Hanley presents both as genuinely true and in tension
Wealth's limits mean wealth is worthless Since wealth can't satisfy every want, pursuing it is pointless Wealth supplies some but not all of what we need and want
