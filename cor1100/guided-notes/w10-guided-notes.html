Part IV
Common Pitfalls Across This Week
These are the most common ways students flatten or misapply the three chapters above.
Pitfall What students assume What the text actually claims How to avoid it
Treating the impartial spectator as "asking someone else" The impartial spectator is a real external person you consult It's an imagined, internalized third position, not an actual other person
Missing one of the two reasons we can't judge ourselves Self-interest alone (or self-delusion alone) explains the problem Smith gives two distinct, compounding reasons
Softening "in no respect better" This means "we're all equally valuable" in a generic, feel-good sense Smith intends a specifically humbling, comparative claim
Treating discipline as a one-time decision Accepting the lesson intellectually is enough Smith insists on repeated, practiced conformity to the impartial spectator's view
Conflating talents with outcomes Smith claims everyone ends up equally successful The claim is about starting natural talent; habit, custom, and education produce real later differences
Treating Plato's "noble lie" as Plato's sincere belief Socrates himself believed the gold/iron-souls story Socrates explicitly calls it a lie; later readers misread it as literal truth
