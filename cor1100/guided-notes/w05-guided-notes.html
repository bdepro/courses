Common Pitfalls Across This Week
These are the most common ways students flatten or misapply the three chapters above.
Pitfall What students assume What the text actually claims How to avoid it
Resolving the two-sided quote into one side Smith's real view is either "necessary for order" or "corrupts sentiments" Hanley presents both as simultaneously true
Missing the connection back to Ch. 2 The relational cost is a freestanding new claim It directly conflicts with TMS's opening line about others' happiness being "necessary"
Reading the chapter's quote as purely self-interested advice "Friends restore your tranquility" means friendship is just a mood-fix tool Smith's friendship is a two-way street; instrumental use defeats the purpose
Conflating the two categories of activities Any shared activity counts equally as a "friendship activity" Some could be done alone; others require mutual engagement to exist at all
Defaulting to romantic love "Love" in this chapter means romantic love Smith means neighbor love/charity, not romance
Treating sympathetic indignation as a contradiction This passage undermines the chapter's main claim It's an explicit, narrow exception to a rule that otherwise still holds
