Part IV
Common Pitfalls Across This Week
These are the most common ways students flatten or misapply the three chapters above.
Pitfall What students assume What the text actually claims How to avoid it
Conflating absolute and relative perfection, or thinking this chapter coins the distinction The two standards are the same thing, or Ch. 23 is where they're first defined Ch. 22 already introduced both terms; only absolute perfection occupies the wise person's "principal attention" here
Assuming the idea of perfection requires special access Only the gifted or specially trained can access it Smith says the idea "exists in the mind of every man"
Confusing self-judgment by absolute perfection with low self-esteem This humility means the wise person actually performs poorly It's the standard, not his actual competence, producing humility
Resolving Ch. 24's closing question prematurely The chapter already explained what's good for the wise person themselves This is explicitly left open until Ch. 25
Treating "highest pleasure" as just more of the same pleasure Self-approbation's pleasure is the same as earlier chapters' attention/recognition pleasures This chapter contrasts them as different in kind, not degree
Reading anxiety about praise as itself praiseworthy Caring about recognition shows proper humility Smith explicitly calls this anxiety "a mark... of some degree of weakness"
