How will your learning be assessed?

Check-Ins 50%
Friday Focus 25%
Economic Puzzles 15%
Monday Morning Economist 10%

  • Four times during the semester you will take a Check-In in class on Wednesday — pen and paper, 60 minutes, full class period. Each one covers the chapters from recent sessions, plus supply and demand analysis and the eight guideposts of economic thinking from Chapter 1. Those two threads appear on every Check-In all semester, so keep them sharp no matter what else is on the schedule.

    Check-In 4 is offered on the last day of class, Friday, December 4. If you need more time to prepare, you can defer it to the final exam period. Either way, it counts.

    Check-In 4 always counts. If your score on Check-In 4 is higher than your lowest score from Check-Ins 1, 2, or 3, I replace that score automatically — you don't need to ask. That means Check-In 4 is worth taking seriously even if the semester has gone well. If you miss a Check-In, CI 4 is the makeup. There are no separate makeup exams. CI 4 covers the full course, so it's the fairest measure of where you land.

  • Friday Focus asks one question: what does economics explain about something you actually care about? You spend the semester building your answer.

    The first part is a written narrative submitted as a Canvas assignment, due Monday, November 30 at 11:59 a.m. Write it as an argument about a real economic phenomenon, for a reader who has never taken economics. You have the whole semester to work on it, and three Friday class sessions set aside for that purpose. Come to those sessions with something on the page.

    The second part is a viva — a short conversation with me about what you wrote. You sign up for a slot during viva week, November 30 through December 4. Think of it as a chance to talk through your argument and explain the choices you made. Sign up early. Slots fill.

    Sign up for your viva slot before Thanksgiving break.
    Use a computer — the Canvas scheduler does not work reliably on phones. No computer access? Contact me and I will assign your slot.

  • Each content session has one Economic Puzzle — a Canvas quiz due Monday at 11:59 a.m. The quiz applies concepts from that session: you read something, work through a problem, or analyze a scenario. The goal is to use the ideas, not catalog them.

    You have two attempts on each puzzle. Use the first attempt after you have done the reading. Use the second after you have thought carefully about where you went wrong. Canvas shows your score between attempts but not which questions you missed — so attempt two rewards genuine re-engagement, not answer-hunting.

    I drop your lowest puzzle score at the end of the semester — one bad week does not sink your grade. If you miss the Monday deadline, you have one more week to submit for 80% credit. After that, the puzzle closes. A rough week is not a reason to stop submitting.

  • Four times during the semester you will read and annotate a current economics article through Perusall — one per check-in block. The Canvas link takes you straight to Perusall. No separate account needed. I introduce each article in class on the Friday of the check-in week. It is due the following Monday at 11:59 a.m.

    The name matters. A Monday Morning Economist reads the news like an economist: looking for incentives, tradeoffs, and unintended consequences rather than just outcomes. Your annotations should show that kind of thinking. A strong response pushes back on the article, asks what the author left out, or connects the argument to something from the course. A weak response restates what the article already said.
