Section IV  LT 2
Subsidies and Pitfalls to Avoid
A subsidy is the mirror image of a tax — same elasticity logic, opposite direction. The pitfalls below are the errors that trip up students across this entire chapter.
Subsidies — the mirror image of a tax

A subsidy splits its benefit between buyers and sellers by the same elasticity logic that splits a tax's burden. You're a policy analyst: a new subsidy pays $30 toward each college tuition bill, regardless of whether the check goes to the student or the school.

Who actually captures more of that $30 — and what determines it, regardless of who the check is made out to?

Pitfall The error How to avoid it
Statutory incidence = actual incidence Assuming whoever legally pays a tax is whoever economically bears it
Revenue-maximizing rate = optimal rate Treating the top of the Laffer curve as the best policy choice, ignoring excess burden
Inelastic = larger deadweight loss Confusing who bears the burden (inelastic side) with who causes the deadweight loss (driven by elasticity, not inelasticity)
"Fixing the price" = no further effects Treating a ceiling or floor as the end of the story instead of the start of non-price rationing and quality effects
Subsidy benefit flows to the named recipient Assuming whoever receives the subsidy check is whoever keeps the benefit
