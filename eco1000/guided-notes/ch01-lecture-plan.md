## Session 1 — Scarcity makes choice unavoidable — and that single fact drives everything else in this course.

**Act function:** Setting
**Big idea:** When resources are limited, every allocation is a forced choice — and changing how you allocate doesn't eliminate the choice problem.
**Guided notes sections:** Key Terms table + Section I (Cornell two-column)

| Time | Activity | Instructor note |
|---|---|---|
| 0:00–0:08 | Open — Organ Donor Puzzle | Pose: "100,000 people are waiting for a kidney right now. Thirteen die today. How do you decide who gets the next one?" Take two hands. Don't resolve. |
| 0:08–0:43 | Build — Section I (Cornell two-column) | Teach scarcity vs. poverty, rationing methods, competition as rationing's shadow. Students fill in the Notes column of Section I as you go. Pre-fill cue questions on slides. |
| 0:43–0:55 | Turn — Ski Resort Pair-Share | "Many ski resorts replaced first-come lift lines with a reservation app for powder days. Pairs: how did the form of competition for a lift-line spot change? Who won? Who lost?" 4 minutes pairs, 4 minutes share-out. |
| 0:55–1:03 | Reflect — Key Terms from Memory | Students close notes. On the back: write definitions for scarcity, rationing, opportunity cost, ceteris paribus from memory. 5 minutes. Open and check. |
| 1:03–1:10 | Bridge | "Uber raised surge prices during a blizzard in Boston. People were furious. Were they right to be? Wednesday." |

## Session 2 — Four principles separate economic thinking from intuition — and each one predicts behavior that surprises people who skip them.

**Act function:** Complication
**Big idea:** Trade-offs, purposeful choice, incentives, and marginal thinking are non-obvious — and most policy arguments skip all four.
**Guided notes sections:** Section II (rows 1–4) + synthesis prompt

| Time | Activity | Instructor note |
|---|---|---|
| 0:00–0:08 | Open — Retrieval + Uber | Generation task: "Without looking at your notes, give me a real-world example — not one from Monday — where you could eliminate poverty but not scarcity. One minute. Write it down." Take two examples aloud. Then pose the Uber blizzard scenario: "What happened to driver supply, and why?" Don't answer yet. |
| 0:08–0:45 | Build — Guideposts 1–4 (Section II, rows 1–4) | Teach one guidepost per scenario. Hospital budget → G1 (opportunity cost). Hiring wage → G2 (purposeful choice). Uber surge → G3 (incentives). Amazon's 101st hire → G4 (marginal thinking). Students fill in each row before moving to the next. 9 minutes per guidepost. |
| 0:45–0:56 | Turn — Bottled-Water Price Cap (Phase 1 + 2) | Phase 1 (4 min): pairs complete consequence 1 (incentives) and consequence 2 (secondary effects) from the synthesis prompt. Phase 2 (5 min): individually, students write the cross-guidepost tension answer. One student shares the tension aloud (2 min). |
| 0:56–1:04 | Reflect — In My Own Words | Students write the summary box: main idea, hardest concept, one real-world connection. 5 minutes silent. |
| 1:04–1:07 | Bridge | "You have four of the economist's tools. On Friday: the other four — and why even the full toolkit can lead you astray." |

## Session 3 — Economic reasoning fails in predictable ways — knowing them in advance is what separates rigor from confidence.

**Act function:** Resolution
**Big idea:** The toolkit has failure modes. G5–G8 describe them; the pitfalls are what happens when you ignore them.
**Guided notes sections:** Section II (rows 5–8) + Section III + Section IV + Self-Test + Reflect & Apply

| Time | Activity | Instructor note |
|---|---|---|
| 0:00–0:10 | Open — Cumulative Retrieval | Cold-call two questions: (1) "Name a rationing method other than price and explain how it changes competition." (2) "Name one incentive effect of rent control." Let the room respond before correcting. |
| 0:10–0:22 | Build — Guideposts 5–8 (Section II, rows 5–8) | Teach G5–G8 instructor-led. Target rollout decision (G5). SF minimum wage secondary effects (G6). Sneaker resale (G7). Fed interest rate claim (G8). Students fill in each row as you go. 3 minutes per guidepost. |
| 0:22–0:32 | Build — Positive vs. Normative (Section III) | Bridge from guideposts: "G7 says value is subjective. G8 says theories must be testable. The positive/normative distinction follows directly — normative statements make value judgments that can't be tested; positive statements make factual claims that can." Walk through Section III matrix. Classify the two pre-loaded rows together. Students fill in their own example. |
| 0:32–0:52 | Build — Four Pitfalls (Section IV) | Bridge from guideposts: "G6 explains why good intentions go wrong. G8 explains why association doesn't prove causation. Here's what that looks like." Read the pre-filled example for each pitfall. Cold-call: "How do you avoid this?" One row at a time. 5 minutes per pitfall. |
| 0:52–1:02 | Turn — Emergency Management Policy Analyst | Students write the Reflect & Apply prompt: one-paragraph briefing (positive analysis + normative recommendation) + which pitfall the governor is most likely to commit. 7 minutes silent write. Two volunteers share. |
| 1:02–1:07 | Reflect — Cover & Recite | Students work the Self-Test section: cover the Notes column, quiz themselves from the Questions column. Mark anything incomplete with ★. |
| 1:07–1:10 | Bridge — Course Arc | "Why do prices exist at all — and what would happen if we removed them? Chapter 2." |

## Chapter Arc Summary

**Dramatic question:** Why does trying to help always cost someone something — and what would it take to reason through that honestly?

| Session | Act | Headline | Students leave believing… |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 (Mon) | Setting | Scarcity makes choice unavoidable. | Every allocation problem is a forced choice. You can change the rationing method; you can't escape the rationing problem. |
| 2 (Wed) | Complication | Four principles separate economic thinking from intuition. | Trade-offs, purposeful choice, incentives, and marginal thinking are non-obvious. Most policy arguments skip them. |
| 3 (Fri) | Resolution | Economic reasoning fails in predictable ways. | The toolkit has edges. G5–G8 describe them; positive/normative and the four pitfalls are what happens when you ignore them. |

**Retrieval architecture:**
- Session 2 opens with: generation task — novel example of poverty without scarcity
- Session 3 opens with: two cold-call questions drawing on Sessions 1 and 2 (rationing + incentives)

**Guidepost split:**
- Session 2: G1–G4 (decision-making cluster — trade-offs, purposeful choice, incentives, marginal thinking)
- Session 3: G5–G8 (limits cluster — information costs, secondary effects, subjective value, testability)

**G5–G8 → Pitfalls bridge (Session 3):**
- G6 (secondary effects) → "good intentions ≠ good outcomes" pitfall
- G8 (testability) → "association ≠ causation" pitfall
- State both links explicitly before opening Section IV
