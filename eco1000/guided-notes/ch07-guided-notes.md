**Bridging national income to full GDP:**

National income only counts money that actually lands in someone's pocket — wages, rents, interest, profit. But GDP counts the full value of everything produced, including some costs that never become anyone's paycheck. Three things close that gap.

Plain-language walkthrough — fill in the blanks:
- A company's machines wear out making this year's products. That wear-and-tear cost is real, but it isn't anyone's income. We call it _____.
- A sales tax or excise tax raises the price you pay without becoming income for the seller — it goes to the government instead. We call this _____.
- A foreign-owned company's factory operates inside this country. Its profits count toward this country's GDP even though they ultimately belong to people in another country. The flip side: money this country's residents earn working abroad counts toward GDP somewhere else, not here. The adjustment for this difference is called _____.

**So:** GDP = national income + indirect business taxes + depreciation + net income of foreigners.

Applied: Shopify pays $600,000 in wages, earns $150,000 in profit, and pays $40,000 in taxes that raise its prices. Which of these belongs in *national income*, and which is one of the three bridge items above? _________________________________________________
