Bridging national income to full GDP

National income only counts money that actually lands in someone's pocket — wages, rents, interest, profit. But GDP counts the full value of everything produced, including some costs that never become anyone's paycheck. Three things close that gap.

A company's machines wear out making this year's products. That wear-and-tear cost is real, but it isn't anyone's income. We call it .

A Quick Anchor — How Much Has Real GDP Actually Grown?

Real GDP per person in the United States has nearly tripled since 1960. Here's a way to feel that number instead of just reading it: in 1930, even a millionaire could not have bought the bundle of goods and services an average American buys today — the products didn't exist yet, or weren't good enough.
