## Section IV — Subsidies and Pitfalls to Avoid

**Subsidies — the mirror image of a tax:**

A subsidy splits its *benefit* between buyers and sellers by the same elasticity logic that splits a tax's *burden*. You're a policy analyst: a new subsidy pays $30 toward each college tuition bill, regardless of whether the check goes to the student or the school.

Who actually captures more of that $30 — and what determines it, regardless of who the check is made out to? _________________________________________________

*Comparison matrix — 5 pitfalls*

| Pitfall | The error | How to avoid it |
|---|---|---|
| Statutory incidence = actual incidence | Assuming whoever legally pays a tax is whoever economically bears it | |
| Revenue-maximizing rate = optimal rate | Treating the top of the Laffer curve as the best policy choice, ignoring excess burden | |
| Inelastic = larger deadweight loss | Confusing who bears the *burden* (inelastic side) with who causes the *deadweight loss* (driven by elasticity, not inelasticity) | |
| "Fixing the price" = no further effects | Treating a ceiling or floor as the end of the story instead of the start of non-price rationing and quality effects | |
| Subsidy benefit flows to the named recipient | Assuming whoever receives the subsidy check is whoever keeps the benefit | |

## Agent 4 — Reviewer Notes (not for students)

**Improvement 1 (Highest Impact): Objective 4.1 (resource-market linkage) is only a fill-in-the-blank, not a worked cue**

The Connection to Prior Learning line references resource-market linkage but gives students no scaffolded path to actually reason through it — unlike every other connection note in Ch. 1/3, which pairs the bridge with a concrete cue. **The fix:** add one Section I row before the price-ceiling cue: "An increase in apartment demand raises the demand for carpenters and electricians who build them. Trace this chain: rising demand in one market → ___ in resource markets → ___."

**Improvement 2: The subsidy section has no graph workspace, unlike every other major model in this chapter**

Price controls and the Laffer curve both get a labeled draw-here block; subsidies — explicitly framed as "the mirror image of a tax" — do not, breaking the parallel structure Biggers & Luo (2020) identify as critical for non-linear formats. **The fix:** add a compact graph workspace under Section IV mirroring the tax-wedge diagram, with "subsidy wedge" replacing "tax wedge."

**Improvement 3: No cue asks students to apply the chapter's central claim (elasticity, not legal assignment, determines burden) to a case where their intuition will be wrong**

Every example chosen (drug with no substitutes, easily-switching rideshare drivers) already points toward the "correct" elasticity story. Transfer-appropriate processing requires at least one case designed to trigger the misconception before correcting it. **The fix:** add a cue where a tax is statutorily placed on the side intuition says "should" pay (e.g., a tax on landlords intended to "make landlords pay"), then ask students to predict the actual split.

**Overall Assessment**: The chapter's strongest feature is the parallel "mirror image" framing connecting tax incidence and subsidy benefit — it lets one elasticity argument do double duty instead of teaching two separate models. Preserve that framing. The three gaps above are coverage and parallelism fixes, not structural problems.
