## Learning Targets
By the end of this lecture, I should be able to…

1. **(LT1)** Apply the laws of demand and supply to predict how buyers and sellers respond to a price change.
2. **(LT2)** Distinguish a change in demand/supply (a shift) from a change in quantity demanded/supplied (a movement), and identify which one a real scenario describes.
3. **(LT3)** Analyze how a market moves toward equilibrium, and evaluate why that point is also the most efficient outcome.
4. **(LT4)** Predict how a shift in demand or supply changes equilibrium price and quantity.
5. **(LT5)** Defend why market prices — without a planner — coordinate buyers' and sellers' decisions.

## Agent 4 — Reviewer Notes (not for students)

**Improvement 1 (Highest Impact): The shifter tables stay flat at Remember/Understand.**

The "Direction it pushes demand/supply" column in Section II only asks students to recall a fact. Nothing in Section II requires Apply or above except the one synthesis prompt at the bottom, after ten rows of pure recall.

**The problem:** Biggers & Luo's Finding 4 says guided notes pay off most on complex, multi-step content — ten rows of single-fact recall is the low-complexity pattern their one counterexample found weak.

**The evidence:** Biggers & Luo (2020), Finding 4 — raise Bloom's level when content is comparatively simple rather than coast on recall.

**The fix:** Convert at least 2 of the 10 shifter rows into a predict-the-combined-outcome cue (e.g., "If buyer income falls *and* a substitute good gets cheaper at the same time, can you tell what happens to price and quantity, or is it ambiguous?") instead of single-direction recall.

---

**Improvement 2: The Pitfalls Matrix repeats Section III's content without adding a new cognitive task.**

Rows 1–3 of the Pitfalls Matrix (shift/movement confusion, equilibrium-as-max-revenue, price-cap-raises-surplus) overlap conceptually with Section III and Section I's efficiency cue. Rows 4–5 (co-movement diagnosis, profit-signal scope) are genuinely new.

**The problem:** Three different sections drilling the same two ideas is redundant load, not reinforcement.

**The evidence:** Cognitive Load Theory — extraneous load from redundant task format competes with genuinely new content for the same limited working memory.

**The fix:** Cut Pitfalls rows 1–3; keep rows 4–5, and add one new pitfall not tested elsewhere (e.g., confusing producer surplus direction when price falls).

---

**Improvement 3: No transfer check between Section I's predict cues and the comparison-matrix terminology.**

Section I asks students to predict behavior in plain language but never asks them to label the prediction using "change in demand" vs. "change in quantity demanded" — that naming task is isolated to Section III.

**The problem:** A student who predicts correctly in Section I may still freeze on a quiz item that requires naming which term applies, since the chapter's formative quiz bank tests label-application directly.

**The evidence:** Transfer-Appropriate Processing — practice tasks should match the cognitive format of the assessment.

**The fix:** Add a labeling clause to two of Section I's cues (already done in this draft — confirm it survives any further edits).

---

**Overall Assessment:** The strongest design choice is the Section III/Pitfalls split naming the shift-vs-movement distinction directly rather than burying it in prose — this chapter has a famous, well-documented common error, and the template confronts it head-on. Preserve that regardless of other revisions; Improvement 2 is about *where* that reasoning is tested, not whether it should be tested at all.
