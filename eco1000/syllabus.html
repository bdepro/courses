Section 2 Why Should You Take This Course?

Everyday life has a hidden structure. Economic thinking is the tool that reveals it.

Why do rents rise when supply stays flat? Why do well-meaning policies sometimes make problems worse? Why do wages differ for work that looks the same? In The Economic Conversation, Klamer, McCloskey, and Ziliak write that studying economics "can help you understand how your own livelihood is connected to the larger economic world" and introduce you to the ongoing conversation about how to make the world more fair and prosperous.

A semester of economic thinking will change how you read the news, make decisions, and understand the choices around you. Economics is one lens among many — history, sociology, and moral philosophy see things it misses. But learning to think like an economist, and to question economic reasoning, is worth your time.

Section 3 What Will You Be Able to Do?
  • 1 Use the eight guideposts of economic thinking to analyze real decisions, evaluate tradeoffs, and identify unintended consequences.
  • 2 Apply supply and demand analysis to explain prices, shortages, and market outcomes across a range of real contexts.
  • 3 Interpret macroeconomic data — GDP, unemployment, inflation, and growth — and understand what drives them.
  • 4 Examine contested policy questions — trade, inequality, labor markets — using economic reasoning rather than intuition alone.
  • 5 Write and defend an economic argument in plain language, for a reader who has never taken economics.

Check-Ins 50% of Grade

Four pen-and-paper check-ins, each taken in class on a Wednesday — one full class period each. Every check-in covers the chapters from recent sessions, plus supply and demand analysis and the eight guideposts of economic thinking from Chapter 1. Those two threads appear on every check-in all semester. Keep them sharp no matter what else is on the schedule.

Check-In 4 always counts — and it can replace your lowest score. If your CI 4 score is higher than your lowest score from CI 1, 2, or 3, I replace that score automatically. You do not need to ask. If you miss a check-in, CI 4 is the makeup. There are no separate makeup exams. CI 4 covers the full course, so it rewards students who keep working all semester.

Check-In 4 is offered on the last day of class () or during the final exam period — your choice.

Friday Focus 25% of Grade

Friday Focus asks one question: what does economics explain about something you actually care about? You spend the semester building your answer.

Part 1 — The Economic Narrative

A written argument about a real economic phenomenon, written for a reader who has never taken economics. Due . Three Friday class sessions are set aside for drafting — come to those sessions with something on the page.

Part 2 — The Narrative Viva

A 12-minute conversation with me about what you wrote, scheduled during viva week (). Think of it as a chance to talk through your argument, explain your choices, and push your thinking further. Sign up before Thanksgiving. Slots fill.

Sign up for your viva slot before Thanksgiving break. Use a computer — the Canvas scheduler is unreliable on phones. No computer access? Contact me and I will assign your slot.
Economic Puzzles 15% of Grade

One Canvas quiz per content session, due Monday at 11:59 a.m. Each puzzle asks you to apply economic reasoning to a real decision, scenario, or dataset. The goal is using the ideas, not cataloging them.

You have two attempts. Use the first after doing the reading. Use the second after thinking carefully about where you went wrong. Canvas shows your score between attempts but not which questions you missed — attempt two rewards genuine re-engagement, not answer-hunting.

Your lowest puzzle score is dropped at the end of the semester. One bad week does not sink your grade. If you miss the Monday deadline, you have one additional week to submit for 80% credit. After that, the puzzle closes.
Monday Morning Economist 10% of Grade

Four current-events articles annotated through Perusall — one per check-in block. I introduce each article on the Friday of check-in week. It is due the following Monday at 11:59 a.m. The Canvas link takes you straight to Perusall. No separate account needed.

A Monday Morning Economist reads the news like an economist: looking for incentives, tradeoffs, and unintended consequences. Your annotations should show that kind of thinking. Strong responses push back on the article, ask what the author left out, or connect the argument to course concepts. Restating what the article already said is not enough.

Section 9 Attendance & Participation

This is an in-person course. You learn by being here. The sessions build on each other — missing one makes the next harder. I do not take formal attendance, but I notice who is in the room, and the work reflects it. If something comes up, email me before class.

Each class begins with a Wellness Pause — a brief, ungraded reflective check-in. It is not optional. Show up on time.

Screens-down Wednesdays. Wednesday is pen-and-paper day. Laptops, tablets, and phones stay closed and face-down for the full Wednesday session. This is a course-wide practice, not a personal preference. If you have a documented accommodation that requires a device on Wednesdays, bring your letter from the Office of Academic Accommodations and Accessibility before the first Wednesday session. I will make it work.

Monday and Friday sessions use skeleton notes that you print or download in advance. Bring them. I do not provide extra copies in class.

Section 10 Academic Integrity

All work submitted in this course must be your own. Elon's Honor Code applies to every assignment.

What this means in practice. Submitting another student's work as your own, sharing answers on Economic Puzzles or Check-Ins, or presenting AI-generated text as your original writing without disclosure are all violations. Violations may result in a failing grade for the assignment or the course and may be referred to the Honor Board.

If you are ever unsure whether something crosses a line, ask me before you submit — not after. A quick email is always better than a conversation I do not want to have. The full text of Elon's Honor Code is available through the Office of Student Conduct.

Section 11 AI Policy

AI tools are part of how we think, write, and work today. Used well, they help you test ideas, clarify arguments, and catch weak reasoning. Used without thought, they undermine the learning you are here to do — and they produce prose that sounds like everyone else's.

You are welcome to use AI to support your process in this course. The Friday Focus narrative is yours to write. The viva is a live conversation. Check-Ins and Economic Puzzles are completed in real time without AI. Those constraints are not arbitrary — they are where the learning happens.

If you use AI in your writing process: revise the output so it reflects your voice and thinking, make sure it is part of a larger piece that shows your reasoning, and add a brief note explaining specifically how you used it. A blanket disclosure at the bottom of every submission is not enough — be specific. I will explain what that looks like for each assignment.

The full AI policy for this course, including task-by-task guidance, is on the course home page. If you are unsure whether a specific use is appropriate, ask before you submit.
