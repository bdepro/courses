Section 2 Why Should You Take This Course?

Everyday life has a hidden structure. Economic thinking reveals it.

Why are rents high? How do incentives shape the choices you make every day? Why do good intentions sometimes lead to bad outcomes? In The Economic Conversation, Klamer, McCloskey, and Ziliak describe what studying economics can do: it “can help you understand how your own livelihood is connected to the larger economic world, and introduce you to the ongoing conversation — among economists, government officials, business leaders, and ordinary citizens — about how to make the world more fair and rich and prosperous for all.”

A semester of economic thinking will change how you see the news, your choices, and the world around you. Economics is one way of seeing. History, theology, and sociology illuminate things economics cannot. But learning to think economically, and to question it, is worth the semester.

Section 3 What Will You Be Able to Do?
  • 1 Ask useful economic questions (why rents rise, what a minimum wage actually does) and find evidence to answer them.
  • 2 Use economic and ethical reasoning to explain everyday choices and assess policy trade-offs, including cases where good intentions produce bad outcomes.
  • 3 Use data to spot patterns in prices, employment, and growth, and make sense of how things relate in the real world.
  • 4 Apply economic thinking to situations you already know to examine what you assumed was true.
  • 5 Explain economic ideas in plain language so others can follow your thinking and join the conversation.

Section 6 How Will Your Learning Be Assessed?
Check-Ins

You will take four in-class check-ins, each on a Wednesday. Check-In 4 always counts. If your CI 4 score beats your lowest score from Check-Ins 1, 2, or 3, I replace that score automatically. You do not need to ask. If you miss a check-in, CI 4 is the makeup. There are no separate makeup exams.

You may bring one 5×8 index card with notes to each check-in.

If 75% of the class completes the end-of-semester survey, everyone gets one bonus point on Check-In 4.

Friday Focus

Friday Focus asks one question: what does economics explain about something you actually care about? You spend the semester building your answer.

Part 1 — The Economic Narrative

The first part is a written narrative, due at the end of the semester. Write it as an argument about a real economic phenomenon, for a reader who has never taken economics. You have the whole semester to work on it. Three Friday class sessions are set aside for that purpose. Come to those sessions with something on the page.

Part 2 — The Narrative Viva

The second part is a viva, a short conversation with me about what you wrote. You sign up for a slot during viva week. Talk through your argument and explain your choices. Sign up early. Slots fill.

Economic Puzzles

One Economic Puzzle per content session, due Monday at 11:59 a.m. Each quiz applies concepts from that session. The goal is to use the ideas, not catalog them.

You have two attempts. Use the first after the reading. Use the second after you have thought carefully about where you went wrong. Canvas shows your score between attempts but not which questions you missed, so attempt two rewards genuine re-engagement, not answer-hunting.

I drop your lowest puzzle score at the end of the semester. One bad week does not sink your grade. If you miss the Monday deadline, you have one more week to submit for 80% credit. After that, the puzzle closes. A rough week is not a reason to stop submitting.

Monday Morning Economist

The name matters. A Monday Morning Economist reads the news like an economist: looking for incentives, tradeoffs, and unintended consequences rather than outcomes. Your annotations should show that kind of thinking. A strong response pushes back on the article, asks what the author left out, or connects the argument to something from the course. A weak response restates what the article already said.

You will annotate four articles through Perusall, one per check-in block. The Canvas link takes you straight to Perusall. No separate account needed. I introduce each article on the Friday of check-in week. It is due the following Monday at 11:59 a.m.

Section 7 How Can I Use AI in This Class?

AI is part of how we learn, think, and write today. Like a search engine or a calculator, it can help if you use it with care. Used well, AI can give you new ideas, test your thinking, and help you write more clearly. Used without thought, it can take over your voice and weaken your learning. You are here to grow, not to copy.

You may use AI to support your process in this course. But AI should never do the whole job. All assignments must be genuinely yours. Check-Ins and Economic Puzzles are completed in real time, in class or on Canvas, without AI.

If you use AI to help write something: revise the text so it fits your voice, keep it as part of a larger piece that shows your thinking, and add a short note explaining how you used it. We will go over how to do that for each Canvas assignment.

Ask if you are unsure. I would rather talk with you than watch you guess.
