Friday Focus

How to Write Your Economic Narrative

A drafting guide for Friday Focus 1. Read this before you begin writing.

Finding Your Story

Everyone has a moment like this.

Think smaller than you think you should. Not a major life decision. Something like arriving somewhere and finding the line much longer than it should have been, or reaching for something at the store and pausing when you saw the price. A moment when ordinary life showed you its own structure for just a second.

You didn't call it economics when it happened. That's fine. This assignment asks you to go back to that moment and tell it carefully.

Start with what actually happened. Don't find a concept first and then hunt for a story to fit it. The economics will come up in the viva. For now, find the moment.

The Six Questions

Once you have a moment, go through these six questions. Answer them in rough notes first, before you write the narrative. The questions draw out the material you'll build from.

1
What happened?
Write what happened in order, just the facts, and hold the interpretation for later.
Example
I was waiting to buy a concert ticket at the box office. The person ahead of me asked about a ticket for a different section, and the attendant said that section had just sold out. The person left without buying anything. I bought the last ticket in my section.
2
Who was there?
List the people involved, yourself included. A couple of specific details per person is enough for a reader to picture them.
Example
The person ahead of me was a woman about my mother's age, dressed like she had come straight from work. She had a credit card already out.
3
When did this happen?
Be specific about when. Someone reading your narrative should know exactly what kind of afternoon you walked into.
Example
It was a Saturday afternoon in December, two weeks before the show.
4
Where did it happen?
Name the actual place — specific enough that someone could find it on a map. A detail or two about what it looked or felt like will pull you back into the memory, which is how you describe it accurately.
Example
I was standing at the box office window of the Paramount Theater on Franklin Street. The line behind me stretched to the sidewalk.
5
How did it happen?
Describe the manner of what happened. Something like "before I could finish my sentence" gives me something to see.
Example
The whole exchange took about thirty seconds. The attendant said it flatly, without apology. The woman paused, looked at the screen, then just walked away.
6
Why did it happen?
This is the hardest question and the most important. What made each person do what they did at that specific moment? Describe what you saw and what was behind it, and leave the economic labels out.
Weak
She left because the ticket was too expensive.
Strong
She had been willing to pay the full price for the seat she wanted. When that seat was gone, paying the same price for a different seat felt like a worse deal, even though the price had not changed.
What you write for question 6 is the economic content of your narrative. Keep the labels out of the essay, and bring that reasoning to the viva. That's what we'll talk about.

Starting with Place and Time

If you're staring at a blank page, start with place and time.

Write one sentence placing the reader in the specific location, precise enough that someone could find it on a map, and a second about what the place actually looked or felt like.

Those two sentences are your opening. Everything that comes after them already has somewhere to stand.

Strong Opening
"It was a Wednesday evening in late March, and the self-checkout line at the Trader Joe's on Lindbergh Avenue had twelve people in it even though three of the six machines were out of service."

That one sentence places the reader in the moment and signals what needs explaining. That's enough to make them want to keep reading.

Who Did What

Once you have place and time on the page, bring in the people.

Put yourself in the scene first — what you were doing, what you wanted — then bring in whoever else mattered to what happened. You don't need everyone who was there, just the ones whose choices shaped the story.

Describe what each person actually did. Show me what impatience looked like at that specific moment, in that specific line.

Weak
There were a lot of people there and everyone wanted the same thing.
Strong
The man behind me had been checking his phone every thirty seconds. When the attendant announced the last available slot, he stepped forward before I had finished speaking.

How and Why

Most student narratives get place, time, and people right. How and why are harder, and they're where the economic thinking actually lives.

How is about the manner of what happened. Something like "before I could finish my sentence" gives me something to see. "Quickly" or "suddenly" doesn't.

Why is about the motive behind what each person did, and it's the hardest element because it asks you to interpret what you saw.

After you write each action, ask yourself what was driving it. If the answer is a category ("she wanted it," "it was too expensive"), push further. What made this person do this specific thing at this specific moment?

Your First Sentence

Start at the moment something is happening. Everything before it is setup you don't need.

A strong first sentence lands you somewhere specific and makes you need to know what happens next. Or it drops you into something already in motion, and you catch up as you go.

"I have always been interested in economics" tells me you're still in the warm-up. Pull back until something is actually happening.

"The sign said the sale ended Sunday, but on Saturday afternoon there were only three left in my size."
"I had been waiting forty minutes when the person at the front of the line walked away without buying anything."
"My roommate and I wanted the same parking spot and we both knew it."

Each one drops you somewhere and makes you want to know what comes next.

A Note on Length

The assignment is 350 to 600 words. That range is narrower than it looks.

350 words is roughly a page and a half, double-spaced. That's enough room for all six elements if every sentence does something.

600 words is the ceiling; stop as soon as the story is told.

The most common problem I see is starting too early. If you've written several sentences of setup before anything happens, you've started in the wrong place. Pull back until you find the first moment where something is actually occurring.

Find the first sentence where something is happening and delete everything before it.

The Writing and the Viva

In the Narrative Viva, I will ask you to walk me through what happened and what you think was driving it.

The more honestly and specifically you wrote it, the more you will have to say.
