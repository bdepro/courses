Narrative Viva: A Preparation Guide

Read this after you submit your narrative and before your viva.

What is a Narrative Viva?

A viva is a short conversation about a piece of writing you have already completed. The Latin root is viva voce, meaning "with living voice." Universities use it at every level, from introductory courses to doctoral defenses, as a way of going behind the text to the thinking that produced it.

The Narrative Viva is a 12-minute Zoom conversation about the story you submitted for Friday Focus 1. I'll ask you to walk me through what happened and what you think was driving it.

What Happens in the 12 Minutes

I don't follow a rigid script, but the shape holds.

Part 1 approximately 4 minutes
Your Story
We start with the narrative. I'll ask you to take me back to the moment you wrote about.
I'll ask you to put me in the room. Where exactly were you? What do you remember thinking?
Part 2 approximately 4 minutes
The Economics in Your Story
We turn to what is happening economically in your narrative. I want to know how you thought about what you saw.
Expect questions about what was driving the choices and outcomes you described: what made each person do what they did.
Part 3 approximately 4 minutes
Looking Back
We close by stepping back. I'll ask what your story revealed when you looked at it economically, and one question about what three weeks of economics has done to how you see things.
You've spent three weeks looking at the world through an economic lens. Where does it help, and where does it fall short?

What I Am Looking For

Ownership of Your Story
You were there. No one else in the room was. The detail you have is something I can't get from anyone else in this conversation. If your answers are vague about your own story, that's usually a sign you haven't sat with it long enough.
Economic Thinking
The label is secondary. What matters is whether you can explain what someone stood to gain and what made them act. If you can, I'll follow the reasoning wherever it goes.
Honest Engagement
If you don't know the answer to a question, say so. If something catches you off guard, take a moment before you respond. A student who pauses and then says something true is doing better than one who fills the silence with words that sound right. I'm listening to the thinking.
Honest Reflection on Three Weeks of Economics
Tell me what you actually noticed. I'd rather hear "I'm not sure it added much" than a careful non-answer.
"I need a moment" is a complete answer. Say it if you need to.

How to Prepare

Before your viva, do these four things.

1
Read your narrative out loud.
Reading out loud reveals things you miss on the page. When a sentence stops you, that's worth noting.
2
Reconstruct the moment.
Close your eyes and put yourself back in the specific place and time you wrote about. What do you remember? What do you notice that you didn't write down? The more clearly you can recall the moment, the more you'll have to say when I ask about it.
3
Work through the economics.
Without looking at your essay, work through what your story was actually about. What did someone stand to gain or lose, and what did they give up to get it? Was there a moment when a small change in price, time, or effort tipped a decision? Think about who or what brought the pieces together.
If you can't answer one of these, that's worth knowing before we talk. It usually means the narrative isn't grounded economically yet.
4
Ask yourself one question.
"What is the most interesting thing about this story that I didn't write down?" The viva will go somewhere you didn't anticipate. Knowing your story well enough to go further than what you wrote is the only preparation that will actually help.

What "Careful Prose" Means

Careful writing is specific.

"I was at the grocery store" contains a fact. "I was in the cereal aisle at the Harris Teeter on Williamson Avenue on a Sunday morning when the price of eggs had just doubled" contains a scene. The second sentence is harder to write. It's also harder to fake.

Read a sentence from your narrative out loud. Would you say it that way to a friend who wanted to know what happened? If not, find the vague part and make it specific.

Someone who was not there should be able to picture the moment.

What a Strong Viva Looks Like

A strong viva is a conversation. Both of us are working through something.

A student doing well in a viva does these things:

She talks about her story the way she'd describe it to someone who wasn't there. The details are specific enough that you could picture the place.
She explains the economics without labeling it. The example she gives is reasoning: "I knew if I waited, someone else would take it."
"I hadn't thought about it that way. Give me a moment." Working through something hard out loud is exactly what I'm looking for.
When she doesn't know something, she says so rather than filling the silence with words that sound right but don't mean anything.
When a question is hard, she goes back to the specific moment she wrote about. The story is always a way back in.
