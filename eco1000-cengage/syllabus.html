Section 2 Why Should You Take This Course?

Everyday life has a hidden structure. Economic thinking reveals it.

Why are rents high? How do incentives shape the choices you make every day? Why do good intentions sometimes lead to bad outcomes? In The Economic Conversation, Klamer, McCloskey, and Ziliak describe what studying economics can do: it “can help you understand how your own livelihood is connected to the larger economic world, and introduce you to the ongoing conversation — among economists, government officials, business leaders, and ordinary citizens — about how to make the world more fair and rich and prosperous for all.”

A semester of economic thinking will change how you see the news, your choices, and the world around you. Economics is one way of seeing. History, theology, and sociology illuminate things economics cannot. But learning to think economically, and to question it, is worth the semester.

Section 3 What Will You Be Able to Do?
  • 1 Ask useful economic questions (why rents rise, what a minimum wage actually does) and find evidence to answer them.
  • 2 Use economic and ethical reasoning to explain everyday choices and assess policy trade-offs, including cases where good intentions produce bad outcomes.
  • 3 Use data to spot patterns in prices, employment, and growth, and make sense of how things relate in the real world.
  • 4 Apply economic thinking to situations you already know to examine what you assumed was true.
  • 5 Explain economic ideas in plain language so others can follow your thinking and join the conversation.

Section 6 How Will Your Learning Be Assessed?
Economic Puzzles 30% of Grade

The goal is to use the ideas, not catalog them.

Each chapter includes an online puzzle in MindTap's Study It section. You apply economic reasoning to a real decision or scenario. Instant feedback tells you how your thinking holds up. You may attempt each puzzle question three times before the deadline, and your highest score counts. To keep things fair and recognize that life happens, I drop your lowest puzzle score at the end of the semester. If you miss a deadline, you can still submit within a week for up to 80% credit. A rough week is not a reason to stop submitting.

Friday Focus: The Economic Narrative and Narrative Viva 30% of Grade

Friday Focus asks one question: what does economics explain about something you actually care about? You spend the semester building your answer.

Friday Focus uses the progymnasmata — a classical sequence of exercises for building arguments and understanding how language shapes reasoning. You will complete two assignments.

Friday Focus 1: The Economic Narrative

A written submission due . You will tell a true story from your own life — 350 to 600 words — about a moment when you made a choice, encountered a price, or watched a market at work. The story must be specific and lived. You will submit a PDF through Moodle.

Friday Focus 2: The Narrative Viva

A 12-minute live Zoom conversation with your instructor during . You will discuss your narrative. The conversation moves through three acts: your story, the economics underneath it, and reflection. Reflection covers two things — what your story reveals, and one question about how three weeks of economics has changed how you see the world. Sign up for your appointment through Moodle. Sign up early. Slots fill.

Achievement Badges 20% of Grade

At the end of each chapter, you complete a short assessment to show what you have learned. These badges focus on understanding, not memorization. Each badge gives you up to three attempts, and only your highest score counts. Think of them as practice with feedback — a chance to confirm what you know and correct what you missed.

Monday Morning Economist 20% of Grade

The name matters. A Monday Morning Economist reads the news like an economist: looking for incentives, tradeoffs, and unintended consequences rather than outcomes.

Each week you read one short economics newsletter and discuss it with your classmates inside the text using Perusall. These are not textbook readings. Each one takes a real situation and shows you the economic logic you did not know was there. You highlight sentences, write comments, and reply to classmates. Credit reflects steady effort and useful discussion. All three newsletters are available from the first day of class — you may work ahead.

Section 9 How Can I Use AI in This Class?

AI is part of how we learn, think, and write today. Like a search engine or a calculator, it can help if you use it with care. Used well, AI can give you new ideas, test your thinking, and help you write more clearly. Used without thought, it can take over your voice and weaken your learning. You are here to grow, not to copy.

You may use AI to support your process in this course. But AI should never do the whole job. All assignments must be genuinely yours. Achievement Badges and Economic Puzzles are completed in real time, without AI.

If you use AI to help write something: revise the text so it fits your voice, keep it as part of a larger piece that shows your thinking, and add a short note explaining how you used it. We will go over how to do that for each Moodle assignment.

Ask if you are unsure. I would rather talk with you than watch you guess.
