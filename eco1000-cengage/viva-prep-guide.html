Narrative Viva: A Preparation Guide

Read this after you submit your narrative and before your viva.

What is a Narrative Viva?

A viva (pronounced VEE-vah) is a short conversation about a piece of writing you have already completed. The word comes from the Latin viva voce, meaning "with living voice." Universities around the world use it, from undergraduate courses to doctoral defenses, to understand how a writer thinks, not just what they wrote.

Your Narrative Viva is a 12-minute Zoom conversation with me about the economic narrative you submitted for Friday Focus 1. You will not be tested on definitions or asked to recite concepts. You will be asked about your story.

What Happens in the 12 Minutes

The conversation has three parts. I will not follow a rigid script, but the shape is consistent.

Part 1 approximately 4 minutes
Your Story
We start with what you wrote. I will ask you to take me back into the moment you described.
Expect questions like: Where exactly were you? What were you thinking at the time? Why did you start the story where you did?
Part 2 approximately 4 minutes
The Economics in Your Story
We look at what is happening economically in your narrative. I am not looking for vocabulary. I am looking for thinking.
Expect questions that ask you to explain what was driving the choices, trade-offs, and outcomes you described.
Part 3 approximately 4 minutes
Looking Back
We step back from the story and ask what it reveals.
What would you add if you wrote it again? What surprised you when you thought about it economically? Is there a bigger question your story raises?

What I Am Looking For

Three things, in this order.

Genuine Ownership of Your Story
You were there. No one else was. The most important thing you can bring to this conversation is the specific, honest detail that only you have. A vague answer about your own experience means you have not thought deeply about that experience.
Economic Thinking, Not Economic Vocabulary
You do not need to say "incentive" or "marginal analysis" to demonstrate that you think like an economist. You need to be able to explain what someone stood to gain, what they gave up, what tipped the decision, and how the outcome came together. The concepts matter. The labels do not.
Honest Engagement
If you do not know the answer to a question, say so. If a question surprises you, take a moment to think. A student who pauses, reconsiders, and then says something specific is doing better than a student who answers quickly with a rehearsed phrase. I am not timing your responses. I am listening to your thinking.
If you freeze, say so. "I need a moment" is a complete answer.

How to Prepare

Do these four things before your viva. In this order.

1
Read your narrative out loud.
Not silently. Out loud. You will hear things you cannot see on the page. Note anything that feels vague, rushed, or hard to follow. Those are the places the viva will probe.
2
Reconstruct the moment.
Close your laptop. Close your eyes. Put yourself back in the specific place and time you wrote about. What do you see? What do you hear? What are you thinking? What happens next? The more vividly you can recall the experience, the more you will have to say.
3
Answer the four economic questions.
From memory, without notes, answer these questions about your narrative:
  • What did someone stand to gain or lose?
  • What did they give up to get it?
  • Was there a moment when a small change in price, in time, or in effort tipped the decision?
  • Who or what brought the pieces together?
You do not need complete answers. You need honest ones. If you cannot answer one of these questions, that is useful information: it means your narrative may not be grounded economically yet, and you should think about why before the viva.
4
Ask yourself one question.
"What is the most interesting thing about this story that I did not write down?" The viva will almost certainly go somewhere you did not anticipate. The best preparation is not memorizing what you wrote. Prepare to think beyond it.

What "Careful Prose" Means

The assignment asks for careful prose. Here is what that means in practice.

Careful prose is not formal academic writing. It does not use jargon, hedging language, or long sentences designed to sound impressive. It is also not a journal entry or a text message. It sits between the textbook and the diary.

A useful test: read a sentence from your narrative and ask whether you would say it out loud to a friend who was genuinely curious about what happened. If the answer is yes, it is probably careful prose. If it sounds like a textbook, simplify it. If it sounds like a diary, sharpen it.

Specific details make prose careful. "I was at the grocery store" is not careful. "I was in the cereal aisle of the Harris Teeter on Williamson Avenue on a Sunday morning when the price of eggs had just doubled" is careful. The second sentence is harder to write and harder to fake. That is the point.

What a Strong Viva Looks Like

A strong viva is not a performance. It is a conversation where both people are genuinely thinking.

A student who gives a strong viva does these things naturally.

She speaks about her story in specific detail, using the actual names of places, people, and prices rather than generic descriptions.
She connects the economic concepts to her experience without being asked to name them. She says "I knew if I waited, someone else would take it" rather than "this illustrates scarcity."
She thinks out loud when a question is hard. She might say "I hadn't thought about it that way. Let me work through that." This is not a weakness. It is what economic thinking looks like in practice.
She does not fill silence with words that sound confident but say nothing.
She remembers that the viva is about her story. When in doubt, she returns to the specific moment she wrote about.
