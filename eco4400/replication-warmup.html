Warm-Up: The 2x2 DiD, Five Ways

A DiD estimate is four averages and three subtractions before it’s ever a regression. Prove it to yourself here, before applying it to a real policy question next stage.

~60–75 minutes, plan for one lab session
Any software: Excel, Stata, Python, R, whatever you have
Read the provided PDF before you start: Scott Cunningham’s “Diff-in-diff can be written down six ways!” (Scott’s Mixtape, 2026). He shows six: two manual calculations and four regressions. This page has you build five of them yourself — both manual calculations, plus three of the four regressions.

The Idea

Take a treatment group and a control group, each observed before and after some event, and subtract twice. That’s the whole calculation. The regressions economists usually run to estimate a DiD, including the ones you’ll use below, are a different arithmetic route to that same number.

Your job in this warm-up is to prove that to yourself, on a dataset simple enough that the arithmetic has nowhere to hide: state-level homicide rates around the 2006 adoption of “castle doctrine” self-defense laws (Cheng & Hoekstra).

The Data

This dataset is public, and it needs no registration or cleaning. It’s restricted to states that adopted castle doctrine in 2006 (treatment) or hadn’t adopted by the end of the sample window (control), comparing 2005 to 2006: 42 states, 84 rows total.

VariableWhat it is

The Task: Five Ways to the Same Number

1
Manual 2x2, first differences then difference
Compute the average l_homicide in each of the four cells (treat×post), then (y11 − y10) − (y01 − y00). This number is your target for every method below.
2
Manual 2x2, group differences then difference
Same four averages as Method 1, subtracted in the other order: (y11 − y01) − (y10 − y00). The signs move with the terms, so the number doesn’t change — only the order you subtract in does.
3
OLS, treatment × post interaction
Regress l_homicide on treat, post, and their interaction. The coefficient on the interaction term is your DiD estimate — the same OLS estimator as every regression below, just with different right-hand-side variables.
4
Twoway fixed effects
Regress l_homicide on the treat×post interaction, controlling for a dummy for every state and a dummy for year. Still OLS — just with many more right-hand-side variables. The interaction coefficient should still match your target.
5
First-difference regression
For each state, compute the change in l_homicide from 2005 to 2006, then regress that change on treat. Same coefficient again.
Cunningham shows a sixth way — a fourth regression, of the treat-minus-control gap on a post dummy. It’s not here on purpose: it makes the same point Method 2 already makes (the order of subtraction doesn’t matter), just as a regression instead of by hand. If you want to build it yourself as a check, it’s a three-line addition to whichever tool you’re using — ask if you want to try it.

Getting Started, by Software

Excel
Steps 1 and 2 are both AVERAGEIFS once you load castle_warmup.csv, just combined in a different order. Steps 3 and 5 work with Data > Data Analysis > Regression once you build a treat×post helper column. Step 4 (state fixed effects) is the hardest to build by hand. Ask me about the demeaning shortcut before you build all 42 state dummy columns.
Stata
use castle.dta, then summarize/reg/xtreg/reshape. Ask me if you want the exact commands.
Python
pandas.read_stata() reads the .dta directly from the URL above. statsmodels.formula.api.ols handles all three regression specs.

What to Turn In

  • Your code or worksheet, in whatever tool you used.
  • 3–4 sentences: why do all five methods have to produce the same number? What does that tell you about what a DiD estimate actually is?
