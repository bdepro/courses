Section 2 Why Should You Take This Course?

In this course you work as an analyst. Your client this semester is Sustainable Alamance, a Burlington nonprofit that needs to know whether its programs are worth what they cost, and your job is to find the evidence, test it, and turn it into a recommendation they can act on.

Mondays work through economic theory from Managerial Economics (Froeb et al.). The consulting project and independent research fill Wednesdays, and teams that form in the first week stay together through the final presentation in Week 12.

By the end of the semester, you will have a portfolio of economic writing and a live client presentation that shows you can move from a messy real-world problem to a clear recommendation.

Section 6 How Will Your Learning Be Assessed?
Consulting Team Labs Data Intensive Community Engaged

Wednesday lab sessions are where the Sustainable Alamance project gets built. Your team works through the full consulting cycle: scoping the problem, gathering and cleaning data, running analyses, and translating findings into a recommendation your client can use. You receive feedback at structured milestones, not just at the end. The final presentation goes to a live audience in Week 12.

Labs are the primary site for SLO 2 (data collection and ethics) and SLO 4 (communicating findings across modalities). Attendance and preparation matter because teams depend on each other.

Applied Research & Case Analytics Data Intensive

Three assignments develop your analytical skills independently of the consulting project. Each is examined orally after you submit it, either as a presentation to the class or a one-on-one meeting with your instructors.

Journal Replication Project

Take a published study on housing and energy use, re-run the analysis on the original data, and write up what you find. Say plainly where your results match the published estimate and where they don’t. Addresses SLO 3 and SLO 5.

Case Analysis 1 — AIG

You advise AIG’s Board on whether to keep expanding its credit default swap business, working only from a January 2005 information set. Your memo takes a defensible position, applying economic frameworks from the course.

Case Analysis 2 — Strategic Games

Strategic decision-making applied to a real industry setting, connecting the analysis to the game theory concepts from the course.

Applied Economics for the BSBA

Monday sessions work through eight chapters of Managerial Economics (Froeb et al.), organized around three questions every manager faces: how to frame a decision correctly (Ch. 1, Ch. 3), how to out-think a strategic rival (Ch. 15), and how to design incentives when information is hidden and interests diverge — the economics of adverse selection and moral hazard (Ch. 19, Ch. 20) applied to employees, divisions, and supply-chain partners (Ch. 21–23). Each chapter starts with a real business decision and shows how economic reasoning changes it. Problem sets and in-class assessments measure whether you can apply the framework, not just define it.

Section 7 How Can I Use AI in This Class?

AI Conditional Use Policy Statement

When used appropriately, you may employ AI tools to support your learning in this course. For example, using AI tools to quiz you over course material, prompting AI tools to explain or clarify course concepts, or using AI tools as instructed by your professor for a class activity or assignment are all allowable uses. However, using AI tools in ways that bypass learning is not considered appropriate. Submitting AI generated work as your own, using AI tools to answer questions during exams or assessments when prohibited by your professor, or other similar uses of AI are inappropriate and represent Elon Honor Code violations. If you are uncertain if your use of AI tools is appropriate, ask your professor.
