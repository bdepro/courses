Section 2 Course Overview

In this course you work as an analyst. The problems are real: a client needs to understand a market, defend a budget, or choose between options with uncertain outcomes. Your job is to gather the right data, build a model that holds up to scrutiny, and deliver advice that actually helps someone decide.

Most weeks divide cleanly. Mondays cover economic theory from Managerial Economics (Froeb et al.), and Wednesdays are split between your consulting team project and independent research assignments. Teams form in the first week and stay together through the final presentation.

By the end of the semester you will have a short portfolio of work—memos, data analyses, and a live client presentation—that demonstrates you can move from a messy real-world problem to a clear recommendation.

Consulting Team Labs Data Intensive 35%

Wednesday lab sessions are where the Sustainable Alamance project gets built. Your team works through the full consulting cycle: scoping the problem, gathering and cleaning data, running analyses, and translating findings into a recommendation your client can use. You receive feedback at structured milestones, not just at the end. The final presentation goes to a live audience in Week 12.

Labs are the primary site for SLO 2 (data collection and ethics) and SLO 4 (communicating findings across modalities). Attendance and preparation matter because teams depend on each other.

Applied Research & Case Analytics Data Intensive 35%

Three assignments develop your analytical skills independently of the consulting project:

  • Journal Replication Project. Take a published study on housing and energy use, re-run the analysis on the original data, and write up what you find—including where the results hold and where they do not. Addresses SLO 3 and SLO 5.
  • FTC v. Amazon (Harvard Case). Antitrust analysis using the HBP Case Companion. The question is whether Amazon’s pricing practices harm consumers or competition, and your memo needs to take a defensible position.
  • Game Theory in Video Games (Harvard Case). Strategic decision-making applied to an industry where payoffs are measurable and the strategies are explicit.
Applied Economics for the BSBA 30%

Monday sessions work through Managerial Economics (Froeb et al.). Each chapter starts with a real business decision and shows how economic reasoning changes it. Topics include opportunity cost, incentive design, pricing strategy, and strategic games. Problem sets and in-class assessments measure whether you can apply the framework, not just define it.

Section 7 Data Nexus Learning Outcomes

This course addresses four of Elon’s five Data Intensive Student Learning Outcomes. SLO 2 is the anchor—it runs through every consulting lab from Week 1 to the final presentation.

The consulting project requires all three functions within a single analytical workflow.
Section 8 AI in This Course

Use AI. I expect it, and at points I will ask you to use it in specific ways. We will talk in class about what that looks like in practice.

The constraint is simple: no assignment, or any substantial part of one, should be produced entirely by a language model. Your analysis, your judgment, and your voice need to be present in the work. AI is most useful when it pushes back on a draft you already wrote, not when it writes the draft for you.

When you use AI, revise what it produces until the text accurately reflects how you think. You will write a short reflection on your AI use for each major assignment—not to police the tool, but because paying attention to how you used it is part of learning to use it well. If you are unsure whether a particular use is appropriate for an assignment, ask before you submit.
