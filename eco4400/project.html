Sustainable Alamance

Economic Impact Study — Burlington, NC — Fall 2026

Your consulting team project this semester is an economic impact study for Sustainable Alamance, a Burlington, NC nonprofit that runs re-entry programming for people leaving incarceration. The organization’s last economic analysis was completed in 2017.

Those numbers held up well at the time—a recidivism rate of 9.8% against a 45% state average and a return of roughly $12 per dollar donated. But eight years is a long time. Incarceration costs have risen, wages have changed, and the program has grown. Your job is to update the numbers, tighten the methodology, and build materials that a county commissioner can read in ten minutes and defend in a budget meeting.

This project is the primary vehicle for the course’s Data Intensive learning outcomes. Every phase involves real data, documented methodology, and deliverables for an actual client.

Why this matters now. County commissioners and grant reviewers need current, defensible numbers. The 2017 report used a flat 40% economic multiplier and cost figures that are significantly out of date. An updated analysis—with county-specific modeling and current wage data—is the difference between a compelling funding case and a document a skeptic can dismiss in two minutes.

Project Phases

Phase 1

Update the core numbers.

Recalculate recidivism savings using current incarceration rates and public defender costs. Update wage and employment figures from BLS and NC Department of Commerce sources. Rerun the ROI calculation against actual operational costs. Document every step so a skeptical official can follow the methodology without your help.

SLO 2 — Data collection & ethics SLO 3 — Methods & limitations
Phase 2

IMPLAN input-output analysis.

The 2017 report applied a standard 40% economic multiplier. That is a defensible approximation, but it is not Alamance County. You will build a county-specific input-output model in IMPLAN that shows where participant wages go, which local industries they reach, and what tax revenue they generate. The result is a multiplier grounded in local data, not a national average.

SLO 5 — Economic modeling SLO 3 — Interpret results
Phase 3

ACS neighborhood context.

American Community Survey data at the census-tract level shows which neighborhoods Sustainable Alamance serves and what the economic conditions look like there. Mapped against participant zip codes, this gives a decision-maker a picture that a spreadsheet cannot. You will pull tract-level data on income, employment, and housing from the Census Bureau and map it against program service areas.

SLO 5 — Visualize data SLO 4 — Communicate findings
Phase 4

Public-facing deliverables.

The analysis becomes a website, a two-page printed summary for commissioner meetings, and a brand recommendation for the “Second Chance Alamance” identity. All materials are designed so Sustainable Alamance staff can update them without outside help. Your team presents the final work to a live client audience in Week 12.

SLO 4 — Multiple modalities
