Main Case: Did Building Energy Codes Lower Electricity Bills?

Same five-way recipe as the warm-up, applied to an actual policy question with a published estimate to check your work against, and a correction worth thinking about.

45–60 minutes core, plus 20–30 if you attempt the stretch
Stata or Python — scripts provided for both
Read both Holian PDFs before you start: the 2020 Economics Letters paper and the 2021 corrigendum that corrects its headline number. You'll need both for the questions below.

The Setting

Since 1978, California — and eventually 32 other states — adopted building energy codes meant to cut household electricity use in new construction. Nearly fifty years later, policymakers still argue about whether these codes actually delivered the savings they promised, or whether the “green building” story is mostly hope dressed up as policy.

You are a research analyst asked to evaluate this the way an economist would. Comparing old homes to new homes and calling the difference “the effect of the code” won't hold up. Incomes, home sizes, and climate control habits all changed across those same decades, and that comparison can't tell the code's effect apart from everything else moving at once. A real comparison group can: states with similar housing stock that never adopted a code, observed over the same period. That's the DiD design you proved to yourself in the warm-up, now applied to a live question.

The Task: Five Ways to the Same Number

Run code/main-case/main_case.do or code/main-case/main_case.py. The structure is deliberately almost identical to the warm-up:

1
Manual 2x2, first differences then difference
On the collapsed panel, compute the average logCOSTELEC in each of the four treat×post cells, then (y11 − y10) − (y01 − y00). This is your target for every method below.
2
Manual 2x2, group differences then difference
Same four averages as Method 1, subtracted in the other order: (y11 − y01) − (y10 − y00). The signs move with the terms, so the number doesn’t change — only the order you subtract in does.
3
OLS, treatment × post interaction
Regress logCOSTELEC on treat, post, and their interaction. The coefficient on the interaction term is your DiD estimate.
4
Twoway fixed effects
Regress logCOSTELEC on the treat×post interaction, controlling for a dummy for every state and a dummy for post. The interaction coefficient should still match your target.
5
First-difference regression
For each state, compute the change in mean logCOSTELEC from pre to post, then regress that change on treat. Same coefficient again.

Comparing to the Published Estimate

Your five numbers should all land around −0.011. Holian's own published, corrected estimate is −0.0225 to −0.026, roughly double yours. That's because your version treats every state the same regardless of population, while his weights by household (HHWT) and adds controls yours skips, like income and household size. The stretch extension below asks you to close that gap yourself.

When you write this up, name the actual reasons: weighting and missing controls.

Stretch Extension (Optional)

Modify the collapse step to weight by HHWT instead of a plain mean, and/or add controls (logHHINCOME, ROOMS, WHITE, AGE, HHSIZE) to a household-level regression. Rerun. Does your estimate move toward Holian's published number? Report what you changed and where the estimate landed, even if it doesn't reach −0.0225 to −0.026. Running the experiment is what counts here.

The Corrigendum Question

Holian's original 2020 paper reported roughly a 4% effect. He caught his own arithmetic error a year later and corrected it to 2–2.5%, still a real, negative effect, just about half the size he first claimed. In 4–5 sentences: does this change how much you trust the finding? A published author caught and corrected his own error a full year after peer review. What does that tell you about why replication, actually checking someone else's numbers yourself, matters even for work that already cleared review?

Getting Started, by Software

Stata
import delimited, then collapse to the state×post panel, then reg/reshape — same commands as the warm-up, applied after the collapse. Ask me if you want the exact syntax.
Python
pandas.read_csv(), .groupby().agg() for the collapse, then statsmodels.formula.api.ols for all three regression specs — identical structure to the warm-up.

Want to try it in Excel? Collapse first with a PivotTable (rows = STATEFIP, columns = BUILTYR3 grouped into pre/post, values = average logCOSTELEC), then work from that small table exactly like Steps 1 and 2 in the warm-up. Doing this on the raw 150,000-row file directly isn't practical in Excel — collapse first.

Match the five point estimates; skip standard errors for this exercise. The scripts cluster by state under the hood because that's correct practice, but nothing in the deliverables below asks you to report or interpret one.
