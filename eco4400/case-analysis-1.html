Chapter 1: Solving Problems with Economics

Every organization succeeds or fails because of the decisions made by its leaders. Chapter 1 introduces managerial economics as a practical framework for improving those decisions. Rather than judging a decision only by its outcome, managerial economists first seek to understand why the decision was made.

The Rational Actor Paradigm assumes that people generally make the decisions they believe are in their best interest, given the information available to them and the incentives they face. This shifts the consultant’s focus away from assigning blame and toward diagnosing the cause of a poor decision: was it a failure of information, a failure of incentives, or both?

Froeb argues that effective problem-solving requires two steps. This framework will become your consulting process for every Board Packet this semester.

1
Diagnose the Problem
  • Who made the decision?
  • Did the decision maker have enough information to make a good decision?
  • Did the decision maker have the incentive to make a good decision?
2
Improve the Decision (recommend one)
  • Assign the decision to someone with better information or incentives.
  • Give the decision maker better information.
  • Change the decision maker’s incentives.

Client Engagement Brief

Client: American International Group, Inc. (AIG)  ·  Date: January 2005  ·  Engagement Lead: You, Junior Economic Consultant, Elite Economic Advisors

The economy. It is January 2005. The U.S. economy is strong. Homeownership is near record highs, interest rates remain relatively low, and housing prices continue to climb. Mortgage lending has become one of the fastest-growing sectors of the financial industry, creating opportunities for banks, investors, and insurance companies. Investment banks are packaging mortgages into mortgage-backed securities and selling them around the world. Demand is growing, ratings remain strong, and confidence in the housing market is high.

The client situation. AIG has become a market leader by selling credit default swaps that insure mortgage-backed securities. The business is highly profitable, demand continues to grow, and historical losses remain extremely low. From management’s perspective, expanding this business appears to be the right decision. A small group of economists and regulators, however, has started asking questions about rising home prices, weakening lending standards, and increasingly complex financial products. The Board wants an independent recommendation before committing additional capital.

What is a Credit Default Swap (CDS)? Think of a Credit Default Swap as insurance. Instead of insuring a house or a car, AIG is insuring investments backed by home mortgages.
  • If the mortgages perform well, AIG keeps the insurance premiums.
  • If many homeowners stop making their mortgage payments, AIG may have to pay investors for their losses.

Digging Deeper: How CDS Actually Work

The insurance analogy above is a useful starting point, but a CDS is not literally insurance, and the differences matter for your analysis.

The basic structure: A CDS is a contract between two parties. The protection buyer pays a periodic premium (quoted in basis points on a notional amount — the face value of the debt being protected). The protection seller — AIG — collects that premium and agrees to pay the buyer if a defined “credit event” occurs (default, bankruptcy, or restructuring of the referenced mortgage-backed securities). If no credit event happens, AIG simply collects premiums for the life of the contract. That is why the business looked so profitable in January 2005.

Where it stops looking like insurance:

InsuranceCredit Default Swap
Regulated; insurers must hold reserves against expected claimsIn 2005, CDS were largely unregulated as insurance. AIG did not have to hold reserves against payout risk
Requires an insurable interest (you can’t insure your neighbor’s house)“Naked” CDS are allowed. A buyer can purchase protection on debt they don’t own, essentially a bet on default
Claims paid only at an actual loss eventMark-to-market and collateral posting: if the market price of the underlying securities falls, AIG can be required to post collateral immediately, even with zero actual defaults yet
Losses are typically uncorrelated (one house fire doesn’t cause another)MBS-linked CDS exposure is correlated. A nationwide housing downturn hits every underlying security at once
Why this matters for your analysis. AIG’s historical loss models priced default risk, which was genuinely low. But the real exposure is to a liquidity event triggered by mark-to-market losses, a risk that historical default data would not reveal. When you write your marginal cost analysis below, this distinction separates a strong memo from a superficial one: the marginal cost of continued expansion goes beyond expected default losses to include potential collateral-posting and liquidity exposure.

Client Deliverable

Prepare a recommendation memo to AIG’s Board of Directors. This should read as one integrated argument: your cost-benefit analysis in Part 1 should directly inform your diagnosis in Part 2.

How to write this memo (consulting craft).
  • Lead with your recommendation. State your bottom-line recommendation in your first sentence or two, then support it. A Board reads the conclusion first, so don’t make them wait for paragraph six.
  • Address the counterargument. State the strongest case against your recommendation, and either rebut it with evidence or explain what would change your mind. Engaging the other side is what separates consulting from advocacy.
  • Know your audience. The Board can act on capital allocation, risk oversight, and executive incentives. Day-to-day underwriting sits below it, so aim your recommendation at decisions the Board actually controls.
1
Part 1 — Cost-Benefit Analysis
  • What are the opportunity costs of expanding further into this business?
  • What are the expected marginal benefits of continued expansion?
  • What are the expected marginal costs of continued expansion — including costs that may not show up in historical loss data?
2
Part 2 — Decision Diagnosis (use your answers above to inform these)
  • Who is making the decision?
  • Given your marginal cost analysis, do they have enough information to make a good decision?
  • Given your marginal benefit analysis, do they have the right incentives?
  • What is the strongest argument against your recommendation, and how do you address it?
  • Should AIG continue expanding? Defend your recommendation using Chapter 1, the required readings, and at least one specific figure (a rate, index value, or default statistic) drawn from the assigned readings.

Format

  • 1.5–2 pages, single-spaced, addressed to the AIG Board of Directors.
  • Written as a consulting memo: professional tone, clear recommendation, evidence-based.
  • Must cite at least one specific quantitative data point from the required readings. General narrative alone (“housing prices are rising”) is not sufficient.
  • No hindsight: every claim must be defensible using only January 2005 information.

How You’ll Be Graded

  • Recommendation clarity: does the memo state its bottom-line recommendation up front?
  • Diagnostic structure: does the memo correctly identify the decision-maker, information gap, and incentive structure, and connect them to the cost-benefit analysis?
  • Evidence: does it cite at least one specific quantitative figure from the required readings?
  • Counterargument: does it engage the strongest opposing view rather than ignoring it?
  • No hindsight: is every claim defensible using only January 2005 information, with no reference to outcomes, the bailout, or “the crisis”?
Recommendation flexibility. There is no single correct final answer. Different well-reasoned recommendations can all score well. What matters is the quality of your diagnosis and the evidence behind it.
