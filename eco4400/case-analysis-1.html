Chapter 1: Solving Problems with Economics

Every organization succeeds or fails because of the decisions made by its leaders. Chapter 1 introduces managerial economics as a practical framework for improving those decisions. Rather than judging a decision only by its outcome, managerial economists first seek to understand why the decision was made.

The Rational Actor Paradigm assumes that people generally make the decisions they believe are in their best interest, given the information available to them and the incentives they face. This shifts the consultant’s focus away from assigning blame and toward diagnosing the cause of a poor decision: was it a failure of information, a failure of incentives, or both?

Froeb argues that effective problem-solving requires two steps. This framework will become your consulting process for every Board Packet this semester.

1
Diagnose the Problem
  • Who made the decision?
  • Did the decision maker have enough information to make a good decision?
  • Did the decision maker have the incentive to make a good decision?
2
Improve the Decision (recommend one)
  • Assign the decision to someone with better information or incentives.
  • Give the decision maker better information.
  • Change the decision maker’s incentives.

Client Engagement Brief

Client: American International Group, Inc. (AIG)  ·  Date: January 2005  ·  Engagement Lead: You, Junior Economic Consultant, Elite Economic Advisors

The economy. It is January 2005. The U.S. economy is strong. Homeownership is near record highs, interest rates remain relatively low, and housing prices continue to climb. Mortgage lending has become one of the fastest-growing sectors of the financial industry, creating enormous opportunities for banks, investors, and insurance companies. Investment banks are packaging mortgages into mortgage-backed securities and selling them around the world. Demand is growing, ratings remain strong, and confidence in the housing market is high.

The client situation. AIG has become a market leader by selling credit default swaps that provide protection against losses on mortgage-backed securities. The business has grown rapidly over the past several years as demand for mortgage-related investments has surged. Credit default swaps have become one of AIG Financial Products’ most profitable business lines, generating hundreds of millions of dollars in annual revenue while experiencing very few historical losses.

The broader economic environment appears favorable. Mortgage interest rates remain relatively low, homeownership is at record levels, and housing prices continue to rise across much of the United States. Investment banks are packaging mortgages into securities and selling them to investors around the world. Credit rating agencies continue to assign high ratings to many of these investments, reinforcing confidence in the housing market. To many executives and investors, the opportunity appears both profitable and relatively safe.

From management’s perspective, expanding this business seems like the logical next step. Demand continues to increase, competitors are entering the market, and AIG has the expertise, reputation, and financial resources to capture an even larger share of this rapidly growing industry.

Not everyone is convinced, however. A small but growing group of economists, regulators, and market analysts has begun raising questions about rapidly rising home prices, increasingly aggressive mortgage lending practices, and the growing complexity of mortgage-related financial products. While these concerns have not yet changed the prevailing optimism in financial markets, they introduce uncertainty about the risks associated with continued expansion.

Before committing additional capital and approving another round of growth, AIG’s Board of Directors has hired Elite Economic Advisors to provide an independent economic analysis. Your team has been asked to evaluate the available evidence and recommend whether AIG should continue expanding its credit default swap business. Your recommendation must rely only on the information available in January 2005.

What is a Credit Default Swap (CDS)? Think of a Credit Default Swap as insurance. Instead of insuring a house or a car, AIG is insuring investments backed by home mortgages.
  • If the mortgages perform well, AIG keeps the insurance premiums for the life of the contract — which is why the business looked so profitable in January 2005.
  • If many homeowners stop making their mortgage payments, AIG may have to pay investors for their losses.

Where it stops looking like insurance:

InsuranceCredit Default Swap
Regulated; insurers must hold reserves against expected claimsIn 2005, CDS were largely unregulated as insurance. AIG did not have to hold reserves against payout risk
Requires an insurable interest (you can’t insure your neighbor’s house)“Naked” CDS are allowed. A buyer can purchase protection on debt they don’t own, essentially a bet on default
Claims paid only at an actual loss eventMark-to-market and collateral posting: if the market price of the underlying securities falls, AIG can be required to post collateral immediately, even with zero actual defaults yet
Losses are typically uncorrelated (one house fire doesn’t cause another)MBS-linked CDS exposure is correlated. A nationwide housing downturn hits every underlying security at once
Why this matters for your analysis.

AIG’s historical models focused primarily on the risk that homeowners would stop making their mortgage payments. At the time, mortgage defaults were relatively low, so the probability of AIG having to cover large realized losses appeared limited.

However, AIG faced another type of risk that historical default data did not fully capture. The value of mortgage-backed securities could fall even before homeowners actually defaulted. Because many of AIG’s credit default swap contracts required the company to post additional collateral when the market value or credit rating of the insured securities declined, AIG could be required to provide large amounts of cash immediately.

This created a liquidity risk. AIG might still expect the mortgages to perform over time, but it could nevertheless face enormous short-term cash demands as investors requested additional collateral. The danger was therefore not limited to the final losses caused by mortgage defaults — it also included the possibility that AIG would not have enough readily available cash to meet its contractual obligations while waiting for the underlying investments to recover or mature.

This distinction is essential to your marginal cost analysis. A strong recommendation should consider not only the expected cost of future defaults, but also the additional collateral requirements, cash flow pressure, and liquidity exposure created by continued expansion.

The question before the Board: Should AIG continue expanding its exposure to mortgage-backed securities?

Client Deliverable

Prepare a recommendation memo to AIG’s Board of Directors. This should read like a professional consulting memo, not a homework assignment, with one integrated argument, not seven disconnected answers. Your goal is to make one clear recommendation supported by sound economic reasoning. Every section should build toward answering the question before the Board:

Should AIG continue expanding its exposure to mortgage-backed securities?

Your cost-benefit analysis should naturally lead into your diagnosis of the decision and ultimately support your final recommendation.

Memo Format

Your memo should be 2 to 3 pages, single spaced, using 12 point Calibri font with 1 inch margins. Organize your memo using the following headings:

1
Executive Recommendation
Begin with a brief summary of your recommendation. Tell the Board what you recommend and why in two or three sentences.
2
Cost Benefit Analysis
Evaluate the economic tradeoffs associated with continued expansion.
  • What are the opportunity costs of expanding this business?
  • What are the expected marginal benefits of writing additional credit default swap contracts?
  • What are the expected marginal costs of continued expansion? Consider both the risks reflected in historical data and the risks that historical loss data may not capture.
3
Decision Diagnosis
Using your cost-benefit analysis, evaluate the quality of the decision itself.
  • Who is making the decision?
  • Do the decision makers have enough information to make a good decision? Why or why not?
  • Do the decision makers have the right incentives to make a good decision? Explain.
4
Final Recommendation
Should AIG continue expanding its credit default swap business? Defend your recommendation using Chapter 1, the assigned readings, and at least one specific piece of evidence from the supporting materials, such as a mortgage rate, home price index, or default statistic.
5
Consultant Expectations
Write as if you are presenting to AIG’s Board of Directors. Be concise, professional, and persuasive. Avoid simply answering the questions one by one — instead, build a logical argument that connects your evidence to your recommendation. Remember, you are writing in January 2005. Your analysis must rely only on the information available at that time — no spoilers!
